The Back of the Napkin

The Back of The Napkin Solving Problems And Selling Ideas With Pictures

We can solve our business problems with pictures. The Back of the Napkin breaks that statement into three essential questions:

01.What problems ?

Any problem. Any problem we have the ability to articulate at all, we have the ability to articulate more clearly through the use of pictures.

02.What pictures ?

Exceedingly simple pictures. We do not need computers nor artistic training nor talent to do this. We can all do this. The pictures we will draw are as simple as a square, a circle, a stick figure, and an arrow connecting them. If we can draw these shapes, we can draw any idea we have.

03.What people ?

The third question is WHO? You and I. We are all born visual thinkers. Anyone who can see well enough to walk into a room can solve problems with pictures, and the pictures we need to draw are very, very simple ones.

Are simple pictures good enough? Yes...

Yes. Visual thinking is not the same as drawing. Drawing is a big part of visual thinking, but visual thinking takes advantage of all our innate abilities to see, that is seeing with our eyes and with our mind's eyes.

The How ?

The Back of the Napkin introduces a process and a set of tools to help anyone quickly begin solving complex problems with simple pictures.

It is easier to process a picture than it is to process words.

(ii)Visual thinking process - Look, See, Imagine and Show. This is the foundation of good visual thinking :

"Look" involves receiving input and making an initial assessment of what is out there so that we know how to respond. To "look" better, we need to appreciate the four cardinal rules: (i) collect everything possible up front, (ii) lay it all out where you can look at it, (iii) establish the underlying information coordinates, and (iv) practice visual triage

To "see" is to determine which input is worth a more detailed inspection. To "see" sharper, we use the 6-by-6 (<6><6>) framework, which helps us map from what we have seen to what we want to show.

To "imagine" is to manipulate the visuals that have been collected and selected with our eyes "closed." To "imagine" further, we use the tool called SQVID, which forces our brain into visual action, whether we want it to or not

To "show" is to make it all clear. To "show," we will pull everything we gathered from above together and finally, deliver the message or presentation.

The Back of the Napkin in Action

The caffeinated company: Peet's Coffee on the back of a napkin

Although Peet's Coffee started the coffee revolution that gave birth to Starbucks, Peet's had always remained a much smaller, quality-oriented company. Peet's CEO Pat O'Dea wanted to create a picture that would instantly explain to staff and investors how he planned to grow the company without sacrificing the quality it was known for.

Pat O'Dea and Dan Roam worked together to create this "Back of the Napkin" sketch of Peet's operating strategy, and now everyone can see where the company is going.
